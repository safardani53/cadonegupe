Painkillers 'for period pain' cost up to 15 TIMES more than bog-standard ibuprofen

Posted by Reinaldo Massengill on Thursday, April 25, 2024

WOMEN plagued by period cramps are paying over the odds for pain relief, experts have warned.

Some face shelling out an extra £4.20 extra per packet for the SAME drugs.

By opting for standard packets of ibuprofen instead of branded packs, many could make huge savings.

Drugs like Feminax Express and Nurofen Express Period Pain are directly marketed at women, to help relieve cramps and pain.

The active ingredient in both is the anti-inflammatory painkiller, ibuprofen.

The exact same active ingredient in a bog-standard, value packet of ibuprofen available at a fraction of the price in all supermarkets and pharmacies.

Feminax Express costs around £4.50 for 16 tablets, while Nurofen Express Period Pain will set you back about £4 for a 16-pack.

But own-brand packets of ibuprofen at Tesco and Boots cost 35p, the Asda version is just 30p, while Lidl are selling them for 25p - all for 16 tablets.

Dr Sarah Jarvis told The Sun: "On the whole, branded painkillers are no better than normal, cheaper ones."

When it comes to "that time of the month", Dr Jarvis said anti-inflammatory painkillers, like ibuprofen, tend to be the best option, because they target prostaglandins, which cause period pains.

She said the only difference between something like Feminax and the bog-standard ibuprofen is how fast it can work.

Feminax also contains salts known as lysine too, which can speed up how fast the drug takes effect.

Dr Jarvis added: "Some are more rapid working, ibuprofen lysine works more quickly so can be good for toothache or period pains.

"It's probably the difference of it working in 20 minutes or 40 minutes - so about half the time to offer the pain relief."

But, she pointed out, that benefit is only felt the first time you take the drug.

"If you take the drug for a few days to deal with period pains, it's only the first dose that will be faster if you opt for branded drugs," she added.

"After that first dose, if you take it regularly, there will be no difference because you build up."

Professor Helen Stokes-Lampard, chair of the Royal College of GPs, said there's very little evidence any extra ingredients make any difference to how easily or quickly painkillers work.

She told The Sun: "Some branded medicines suggest that different formulations of key ingredients are absorbed faster by the body, but there is limited scientific evidence for this and this is why the NHS doesn't offer these options as a prescription treatment."

She added it's vital women check the backs of packets to ensure they're not getting ripped off.

"Regardless of the packaging or how much you spend on medication, it's the quantity and quality of the actual medical ingredients in that medication that matter most," Prof Stokes-Lampard said.

"We would therefore urge patients to always check the box on any medication before buying it, so they are not paying over the odds to get the treatment they need."

And she said women in doubt should always ask their pharmacist, who will be able to advise if there's a "similar but more cost-effective option".

A spokeswoman for Bayer UK, the firm that makes Feminax, told The Sun most branded medicines for sale are different to "the generic equivalents".

She said: "Although the active ingredient in branded and generic over-the-counter medicines might be the same, the inactive ingredients, which are used to formulate the active ingredient into its given form (eg. tablet or liquid) may differ, causing the medicines to vary in appearance, speed of release or duration of relief."

A spokesman for the Reckitt Benckiser Group, that makes Nurofen, added: "While some pain relief products can seem to be the same, the finished product can still have differences.

"The format or additions to the active ingredient can influence factors such as taste, feel and ease of use."

He added: "Branded medication can be more expensive than generic products due to investment in research and product innovation."

Doctors have warned popping too many painkillers, to relieve period cramps, is a bad idea.

Non-steroidal anti-inflammatories, like ibuprofen can increase the risk of nasty side effects, including stomach ulcers, acid reflux and digestive problems.

In the long run they can cause stomach inflammation, low blood pressure, constipation and diarrhoea.

Dr Sahil Khanna, a gastroenterologist at the Mayo Clinic in the US, said: "Taking them so much can have serious gastrointestinal side effects."

And she warned it's often not until a stomach ulcer ruptures or bleeds that they are noticed.

She added you should never take these painkillers on an empty stomach, and never go over the recommended daily dosage.

Feminax Express contains 342mg of ibuprofen lysine per tablet - "the equivalent to 200mg ibuprofen", it's website states.

It is described as being "for the treatment and relief of the symptoms of periods, including cramps, headaches and other symptoms".

Nurofen Express Period Pain also contains 200mg of ibuprofen, and claims to last for "up to eight hours".
